1. Style & Structure Signals (cheap, practical)

These are easy to implement and don’t require fancy ML right away.

a) Over-templated structure

Look for resumes that follow this ultra-generic pattern:

Summary: “Results-driven [ROLE] with a proven track record of…”

Bullet structure: every line starts with a strong verb + a vague impact.

Section headers all standard: “Professional Summary”, “Experience”, “Education”, “Skills”, “Certifications” with no variation.

No company descriptions, no tech stack specificity, almost no numbers.

What you do in code:

Build a list of common cliché phrases and intros (“results-driven”, “detail-oriented”, “proven track record”, “dynamic professional”, etc.).

Count:

% of sentences containing those phrases

of bullets starting with the exact same verbs (“Led”, “Managed”, “Implemented”) with no variety

If the density crosses some threshold → bump an “AI-style likelihood” score.

b) Overly polished, homogeneous language

LLM resumes often:

Have perfect spelling/grammar across the entire doc.

Use very consistent sentence length and structure.

Avoid “messy” artifacts (typos, fragments, inconsistent formatting).

Implementation ideas:

Run a grammar checker / spell checker:

If zero errors across 2–3 pages → suspicious, especially for junior roles.

Compute:

Average sentence length and variance

Average word length

Type–token ratio (unique words / total words)

Extremely uniform style → increase AI-likelihood score.

You’re not saying “this is AI,” just “this is unusually ‘clean’ compared to typical human resumes.”

2. Semantic Signals (using an LLM/embeddings)
a) Generic vs specific content score

Ask your model:

“On a scale of 0–1, how generic and template-like is this resume, vs specific and grounded in real situations (company names, tools, metrics)?”

Then also check:

% of bullets that contain:

A concrete metric (%, $, #)

A named tool/tech (SQL, Salesforce, Figma, etc.)

A real company or product name

A resume that is:

High genericity

Low specific entities

No metrics
= very likely AI-padded (or written via a template).

Expose that as:

“This resume is highly generic: very few specific metrics, tools, or concrete achievements. It reads like a template rather than a lived work history.”

You don’t need to scream “ChatGPT” – just flag the behavior.

b) “Fluff vs substance” ratio

Use the model to tag each bullet line:

Is this:

Concrete impact (with numbers / clear before–after)?

Vague fluff?

Restatement of responsibilities?

AI-generated resumes often spam fluff to sound impressive.

You can show:

“Fluff ratio: 76% of bullet points are vague and lack measurable outcomes.”

This is useful even if it’s human-written. It’s a quality signal, not just AI detection.

3. Perplexity / “AI-ness” Models (with caveats)

The classic AI detector trick: measure perplexity (how “predictable” the text is for a language model):

AI text tends to be very predictable (low perplexity).

Human text is noisier (higher perplexity).

In practice:

You can send chunks of the resume to a small local model or a hosted endpoint and compute a “AI-like probability” score.

But: these detectors produce false positives, especially on polished corporate writing.

Also: any small re-edit by the candidate can throw them off.

If you implement this, I’d:

Use it as one feature inside a broader “AI-likelihood” score.

Never present it as “This was generated by ChatGPT.”

Show it as “Writing style is highly uniform and machine-like; consider asking clarifying questions.”

4. Template & Duplication Detection (very useful)

You can use your database to detect:

Near-duplicate resumes:

Same structure + phrasing, different names/emails.

High cosine similarity of text embeddings across different candidates.

LLM abuse patterns often show up as:

One person’s resume being cloned and slightly modified for others.

The same bullet language reused across many applicants.

Implementation:

Store an embedding (vector) of each resume.

For each new resume:

Check nearest neighbors in vector space.

If similarity > threshold and identity info differs → “possible template/clone.”

This is less about ChatGPT specifically and more about copy-paste fakery, but your customers will care more about that anyway.

5. Explicit “AI Usage Disclosure” Flow

Instead of only trying to catch AI, you can design your system to normalize honest use:

Add an optional candidate question UI that your client can send:

“Did you use AI tools (e.g., ChatGPT) to help write or format your resume? If yes, how?”

Show that answer in your dashboard.

Then your tool’s value becomes:

Compare declared AI usage vs detected AI-style signals.

Flag mismatches as integrity risk, not “AI bad.”

This reframes AI use as neutral, but lying about it as a trust issue.

6. What You Should NOT Do

Be careful with these:

Hard classification like “This resume was generated by ChatGPT.”
You simply can’t reliably know. That’s how you piss off legit candidates and potentially expose clients to bias claims.

Auto-rejecting AI-suspected resumes.
Terrible idea. Use it as a prompt:

“Resume appears heavily templated; we recommend a skills test or structured interview before proceeding.”

Treating AI usage as inherently bad.
Smart candidates will use AI. The question is: do they have real skills and real history underneath?

Here’s how I’d present it in your app:

Add a panel called:

“Authenticity & Specificity Signals”

Within that:

% cliché phrases

Per-sentence structure variance

Specificity Score

% bullets with metrics

of named tools / tech
of concrete projects / products

Template/Clone Risk

“This resume is 91% similar to 3 other resumes in your database.”

AI-Style Likelihood (soft language)

“The writing style is highly polished and uniform; may have been assisted by AI tools. We recommend deeper probing in interviews.”

So you’re:

Giving intel.

Not pretending to be an AI lie detector god.

8. What Actually Matters to Your Customers

Most employers don’t actually care which tool wrote the resume. They care about:

Is this person real?

Do they actually have the experience they claim?

Can they do the job?

So the killer combo in Résumé Logik is:

Resume logic checks (timeline, overlap, promotions, specifics)

AI-style / genericity indicators

Skills tests you generate based on the resume and job

Instead of “This is ChatGPT,” the much more useful output is:

“This resume is extremely generic and likely AI-assisted. It contains few concrete examples or measurable outcomes. Recommend sending a short skill assessment or asking for a more detailed work history before moving forward.”

That’s actionable and defensible.
